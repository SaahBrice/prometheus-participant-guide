# The 2-minute demo

The video is worth 25% of your total score, and it is the only part of your submission that every judge is guaranteed to experience in full. Most teams treat it as an afterthought recorded in the final hour. Treat it instead as a deliverable with its own time budget, scheduled into the final hours of day two.

## A structure that works

- 0:00 to 0:20. The problem, told through a real person with a real pain point. "Maria teaches 60 students and spends her evenings grading" communicates more than "education has challenges."
- 0:20 to 0:40. Your solution in one sentence, then cut directly to the screen.
- 0:40 to 1:30. The complete flow from start to finish, with no detours. One full journey through the product, using real input and showing real output.
- 1:30 to 1:50. What the AI is doing underneath. Judges score Creative AI/ML Use partly from this segment, so state explicitly what the model does and why the product could not work without it.
- 1:50 to 2:00. The impact in one line: who is better off, and how.

## Rules drawn from watching these fail

- Record the screen with real data. Placeholder text and "test test 123" in the input fields signal an unfinished product.
- Script the narration word for word, then rehearse it until it sounds natural. Improvised narration rambles, and rambling consumes the middle section where the product should be on screen.
- Record in a quiet room with a usable microphone. Reviewers unconsciously read bad audio as a bad product.
- Do not spend 40 seconds on team introductions or market-size slides. Judges want to see the product operating.
- Do not show source code in the video unless the code itself is the product. The repository is where the code lives.
- Record the demo flow at least twice, because something always glitches on the first take.
- Cut until the video fits inside 2:00. Running over the limit signals an inability to prioritize, which is the exact skill this event tests.

## Before you submit

Verify the following: the video is 2 minutes or under and the link is publicly accessible; the repository is public, contains a README with run instructions, and contains no API keys; the prototype runs correctly from a fresh clone; and the submission form is fully completed. Then submit hours before the deadline rather than minutes, because Devpost closes submissions at the deadline with no exceptions.

Good luck. Build one thing properly, explain it clearly, and you are already ahead of most of the field.
