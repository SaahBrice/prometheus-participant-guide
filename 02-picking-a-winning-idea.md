# Picking a winning idea

The winning formula is narrow: one user, one problem, one working flow. In 13 days you cannot build a platform. You can build one thing that works every time and clearly helps someone learn.

## A quick test for your idea

Before writing code, answer these in one sentence each. If you can't, keep thinking.

- Who exactly uses this? ("Students" is not exact. "A secondary-school student revising for exams alone at home" is.)
- What can they do after using it that they couldn't before?
- What is the AI actually doing that a plain app couldn't?
- Can you demo the whole thing in under 90 seconds?

## Directions that fit this hackathon well

These are starting points, not assignments. The winner will be the team that picks one and goes deep.

Agentic AI in education is the richest territory right now. Instead of a chatbot that answers questions, build an agent that pursues a goal across steps: a study coach that plans a revision schedule, quizzes you, notices weak topics, and reshapes the plan; a teaching assistant that takes a syllabus and generates lesson plans, exercises, and marking schemes; a feedback agent that reads a student's essay, identifies the recurring weakness, and drills exactly that.

Personalized learning: tools that adapt pace, difficulty, or explanation style to the individual. The AI's memory of the learner is the product.

Accessibility: turning lectures into notes for deaf students, describing diagrams for blind students, simplifying dense texts for younger readers or non-native speakers, voice-first learning for low-literacy contexts.

Teacher tools: teachers are drowning in grading and prep. Anything that saves a teacher an hour a day has obvious, demonstrable impact — which is exactly what the Educational Impact criterion measures.

Local and underserved contexts: learning in languages that big platforms ignore, exam prep for national curricula, tools that work on cheap phones or bad connections. Judges reward teams who clearly know a context others overlook.

## Smart use of AI, in your product and in your process

In the product: don't just wrap a chatbot. Combine the LLM with structure — your own question bank, a spaced-repetition schedule, retrieval over real course material, a rubric. The structure is what makes it yours; the LLM makes it adaptive.

In your process: using AI assistants to write code is allowed and sensible. But understand every line you ship. Judges read code and may ask questions, and pasted code you can't explain or fix collapses under the Technical Execution criterion. Use AI to go faster at things you understand, not to skip understanding.

## Scope discipline

Write down your feature list on day one, then cut it to the minimum that demonstrates the core idea. When you're tempted to add a feature mid-hackathon, ask: does this appear in the 2-minute video? If not, it does not exist. Finish early and spend the saved time on stability and the pitch — that trade wins hackathons.
